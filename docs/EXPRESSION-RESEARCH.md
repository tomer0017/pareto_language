# READY — Pareto Expression Research (audit only, no content changes)

> A research audit of candidate **conversational expressions** for possible future inclusion. This
> is a **report**, not an implementation: **no Bootcamp content is modified**. Every recommendation
> must later pass the Product Rule before it is built.

## The bar (Product Rule)

> *If READY could teach only 30 missions for the rest of someone's life — would this conversation
> still deserve one of those 30?*

READY is survival-first and Pareto-ruthless. An expression earns a slot only if a near-beginner will
realistically **hear it or need to say it** in a common travel moment, and it is **short and learnable**.
Expressive idioms that a beginner would never produce (and rarely must decode to survive) do **not** qualify.

## Scoring rubric (1–5)

| Axis | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Freq** | How often it actually occurs in real spoken travel interaction |
| **Value** | Communication payoff when known (keeps a conversation alive / avoids a freeze) |
| **Travel** | Usefulness in the ~10 core travel situations |
| **Beginner** | Learnability + likelihood a zero-English traveler can deploy it |
| **Conf** | Confidence in this assessment |

**Include** = Freq ≥4 **and** Value ≥4 **and** Beginner ≥3. Otherwise Backlog or Reject.

## 3. Findings

- The **high-value winners are reactive "glue" replies**, not idioms: *Sounds good · No worries ·
  That's fine · Of course · It's up to you · Never mind · I'm not sure · Maybe later*, plus the
  **hear-only** set *Here you go · Go ahead · Take your time*. They are short, extremely frequent, and
  directly serve READY's "never freeze" philosophy: they keep a conversation moving when the learner
  has understood but doesn't know how to respond.
- **Idioms and expressive phrases fail the bar.** *Fingers crossed / Better safe than sorry / I had
  to pinch myself / You have no idea…* are colorful but a zero-English traveler will neither produce
  them nor need to decode them to survive a café, a taxi, or a border desk. They would burn a scarce
  learning slot for near-zero survival return — a textbook Pareto rejection.
- **Several winners are already partly covered:** *Me too!* (M23 Small Talk), *No, thank you!* (M4/M9),
  *One moment, please.* / *Thank you!* / *Sorry!* (Recovery Toolkit). The genuinely **missing** set is
  the agreement/decision/uncertainty glue: *Sounds good · No worries · That's fine · Of course · It's
  up to you · Never mind · I'm not sure · Maybe later*, and the hear-first *Here you go · Go ahead ·
  Take your time*.

## 4. Recommendation (for a FUTURE sprint — not this one)

Do **not** create a new numbered mission for these. Applying the Product Rule strictly: a standalone
"expressions" mission does **not** beat the current 30 for one of the slots, and **Mission 23 (Small
Talk)** and the **Recovery Toolkit** already own this territory. Instead, when approved:

1. **Extend Mission 23 (Small Talk)** with a short "reactive replies" beat: *Sounds good · That's
   fine · Of course · It's up to you · I'm not sure · Maybe later* (say-channel) and *Here you go ·
   Go ahead · Take your time* (hear-channel / expected replies).
2. **Add *Never mind* to the Recovery Toolkit** — it is a survival move (gracefully drop a request
   you can't complete), the same family as *Sorry* / *One moment*.
3. **Backlog** the near-duplicates (*That works · Not a problem · Let me think*) — teach one member of
   each cluster only; duplicates dilute without adding coverage.

All of the above must go through the normal content pipeline (Concept Layer → realization → review)
and native Hebrew review; **none is implemented in this sprint.**

## 5. Confidence & limitations

Frequency/value scores are expert estimates, not corpus-measured. Before building, validate the
shortlist against a spoken-frequency source and a native reviewer, and confirm each still clears the
"1 of 30" bar in context. The strongest, lowest-risk additions are *Sounds good*, *No worries*,
*That's fine*, and *Here you go* (hear).
